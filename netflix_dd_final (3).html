The Daily Dividend Take
Netflix spent a decade losing money to build the most valuable streaming library on earth. Now they are printing cash. The $39B in revenue is impressive. The $6.9B in actual cash profit is what changes the story. The next five years are not about American subscribers. They are about figuring out how to get a billion people in India and Southeast Asia to pay for something they currently access for under $2 a month. The ad-supported tier is the only credible bridge. If they build it right, this company looks very different in 2030.

How Netflix makes money
The idea is simple: pay a monthly fee and watch whatever you want. Netflix keeps that fee whether you watch 10 hours or nothing at all. That is $39B in income they know is coming before they spend a single dollar making shows. It flows in through three doors.

Advertising
$2B+ and growing fast
New engine
In 2022 Netflix launched a cheaper plan that shows ads. People who cannot afford $15 a month can now access Netflix for $7 and watch ads instead. Netflix earns from both sides: the subscriber pays $7 and brands pay Netflix to show their ads to that viewer. Brands pay more to advertise on Netflix than almost anywhere else online because Netflix viewers are engaged, not just scrolling past. For every 1,000 people who see an ad, Netflix charges advertisers $30 to $60. For comparison, YouTube charges roughly $10. Netflix viewers are considered premium so brands pay more to reach them.

The India problem and the India opportunity
Netflix charges about $1.80 a month in India. In the US, the cheapest plan is $7. The gap is enormous. The ad-supported plan is the best chance Netflix has to close it. If brands want to reach Indian viewers and pay Netflix to show them ads, Netflix earns money even when the subscriber is paying almost nothing. Crack this and Asia Pacific goes from a footnote to the biggest growth story of the decade.

The Daily Dividend Take
The number that matters most is not $39B in revenue. It is $46M a day on content and the fact that a Korean show budgeted at $21M was watched by more people than most Hollywood blockbusters will ever reach. Netflix's edge is not money. It is the discovery that the best stories do not always come from the most expensive places.
The Signals
What the data is telling you
Green flags
+
Password crackdown beat every estimate
Netflix forced millions of people sharing accounts to start paying. They added 19 million new paying subscribers. Most analysts expected people to just cancel instead. They did not.
+
The ad plan is quietly becoming massive
40 million people now pay $7 a month and watch ads. Netflix earns from the subscriber and from the brand paying to show that ad. Two revenue streams from one viewer.
+
Making more money from the same subscribers
Netflix's profit margin jumped from 21% to 26% in one year. The cost of making shows barely changed. Revenue grew. That gap goes straight to profit.
+
Local shows are the real competitive edge
Squid Game, Money Heist, Dark. Cheap to make by Hollywood standards. Watched by hundreds of millions. Disney and Amazon cannot replicate a Korean cultural moment.
+
Live sport keeps subscribers from cancelling
People used to cancel Netflix in January when there was nothing new to watch. WWE, NFL, and boxing matches now give them a reason to stay every month of the year.
Red flags
–
Asia: enormous audience, tiny revenue
60% of the world's population lives in Asia. Netflix makes only 12% of its revenue there. The monthly fee they can charge in India is around $1.80 versus $17 in the US. That gap has no easy fix.
–
Disney, Apple and Amazon force constant spending
All three competitors are making their own shows. If Netflix stops releasing new content, subscribers leave for the competition. They spent $17 billion on content in 2024 and will spend more in 2025.
–
Nobody can predict which shows will work
They spend $46 million every single day on content and still produce flops alongside the Squid Games. There is no formula. The biggest financial risk in the entire business is content that does not land.
–
The ad business is still being built
Netflix wants to compete with Google and Meta for advertising money. But they are still building the technology to measure and target ads properly while simultaneously trying to sell those ads. That is hard to do at speed.
For the stock to double
What needs to happen
01
Crack the India and Asia problem through ads
Netflix earns about $1.80 per month from an Indian subscriber vs $17 in the US. If brands start paying Netflix to advertise to Asian viewers at scale, that gap closes. Even moving from $1.80 to $5 or $6 per user would add billions in annual revenue. This one move is worth more than adding 50 million new American subscribers.
Potential add: $8 to $12 billion in annual revenue
02
The ad business scales to YouTube size
YouTube makes about $35 billion a year from advertising. Netflix charges brands more per viewer than YouTube does because Netflix viewers are more engaged. But Netflix only has 40 million people on its ad plan today. If that grows to 200 million, and they build the right ad technology, the math becomes extraordinary.
Potential add: $10 billion or more in annual revenue
03
Profit margin keeps expanding without more spending
Making shows costs roughly the same whether Netflix has 250 million or 350 million subscribers. More subscribers means more revenue but not proportionally more cost. If revenue keeps growing at 15% a year while content spending stays flat, the profit grows even faster. Netflix's profit margin was 26% in 2024. At 35% on $50 billion in revenue, the earnings story looks completely different.
Earnings per share could double without a single new subscriber
04
Win a major sports league and become the default screen
NFL, WWE, and boxing are the start. If Netflix wins the rights to show the Premier League, NBA, or Olympics, it becomes the one app every household needs. Live sport is the last thing that still forces people to pay for television. Owning it on streaming changes the entire market.
Each major sports deal likely adds 5 to 15 million subscribers
05
The next Squid Game from India, Nigeria or Brazil
Squid Game cost $21 million and was watched by 265 million accounts in its first month. One show from a new market doing that adds an estimated 10 to 15 million subscribers globally at almost no extra cost. The content library is the pipeline. The model is proven. It just needs to happen again.
Cultural moments add subscribers better than any marketing campaign
