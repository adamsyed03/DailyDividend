The Daily Dividend Take
Visa is the closest thing to a perfect business that exists. It sits in the middle of every payment, takes a small cut, and bears none of the risk. It does not care if you pay your credit card bill. It does not care if the economy slows. As long as money moves, Visa earns. The DOJ lawsuit is the most serious threat in years. But the real question is simpler: will people keep using cards? If yes, Visa wins. The next decade is about whether that question has the same answer in Mumbai, Jakarta, and Nairobi as it does in New York.
The Business
How Visa makes money
Visa is not a bank. It does not give you a credit card or hold your money. It is the road that money travels on. Every card transaction in the world needs to go through a network. Visa built the biggest one. And it charges a small toll every single time.
🏦
Service revenues
~$19.1B per year
Core engine
When you tap your card at a coffee shop, a tiny fraction of a percent of that transaction goes to Visa. It is called a service fee. On its own it sounds like nothing. But Visa processes 800 million transactions a day, so the tiny fees add up to billions. Banks pay Visa to use its network because without it their cards would not work anywhere. Merchants accept Visa cards because their customers have them. It is a loop that is almost impossible to break.

International transaction revenues
~$11.3B per year
Highest margin
When you use your Visa card in a foreign country, or buy something online from a shop based overseas, Visa charges a higher fee. This is called a cross-border fee and it is the most profitable thing Visa does. Think about every holiday booking, every online purchase from a foreign website, every business trip. All of that flows through Visa's international network and generates a premium fee. When international travel recovers and grows, this section of Visa's income grows even faster than the rest.

The China problem and the India opportunity
China is the largest consumer market on earth and Visa barely operates there. UnionPay handles 90%+ of domestic payments and the government has made it clear foreign card networks are not welcome. India is different. Digital payments are booming, government-built payment systems are growing, but international cards are gaining ground on premium purchases. Southeast Asia is even earlier in the journey. The long game here is enormous.

The Daily Dividend Take
The number that defines Visa is not $35.9B in revenue. It is $0 in credit risk. Visa built a business that earns every time money moves and loses nothing when it goes wrong. That is extraordinarily rare. The only threat to this model is a world where money moves without cards. That world is not here yet. And the moment it starts to arrive, Visa will already be building the pipes for it.
The Signals
What the data is telling you
Green flags
+
Cash is dying and Visa wins every funeral
Roughly 80% of global payments are still made in cash. Every country that digitises its economy adds payment volume to Visa's network. India, Brazil, Nigeria, Indonesia. The shift is still in its early stages globally.
+
International travel is the highest-margin product Visa has
When you spend money abroad, Visa charges a higher fee than for a domestic purchase. The more people travel internationally, the faster this part of the business grows. Cross-border volume grew 16% in FY2024.
+
The network is 60 years old and impossible to copy
You cannot just build an alternative to Visa. You need every bank to issue your cards, every merchant to accept them, and every consumer to want them. It took 60 years to get here. No competitor is starting over.
+
New businesses paying with cards, not just consumers
B2B payments — companies paying suppliers, wages, and invoices — are a $125 trillion market that still runs mostly on slow bank transfers and paper cheques. Visa is moving into this space. It is an enormous untapped opportunity.
+
Buying back shares every single year, quietly making each one more valuable
Visa spends billions every year buying back its own shares. Fewer shares in existence means each one you hold represents a bigger slice of the same business. They have done this consistently for 15 years.
Red flags
–
The US government is suing Visa for having a monopoly on debit payments
In September 2024, the Department of Justice filed a lawsuit alleging Visa illegally controls over 60% of US debit card processing, blocking out competitors. A judge refused to dismiss the case in June 2025. This is ongoing and unresolved.
–
Apple, PayPal, and bank apps are building their own payment systems
Apple Pay still runs on Visa rails in most countries. But Apple is experimenting with payment systems that bypass cards entirely. If major tech companies build direct bank-to-bank payment tools, a small number of Visa transactions slowly start disappearing.
–
China is the biggest consumer market on earth, and Visa is barely there
UnionPay, WeChat Pay, and AliPay dominate China. The government has made it difficult for foreign card networks to operate at scale domestically. That is 1.4 billion consumers Visa largely cannot reach.
–
Governments can cap the fees Visa charges — and some are trying to
Merchants have complained for years that Visa's fees are too high. The EU has already capped certain fees. If regulators in the US or UK do the same thing, Visa's revenue per transaction drops. The DOJ lawsuit is partly about this exact issue.
For the stock to double
What needs to happen
01
The B2B payments opportunity gets cracked
Businesses paying other businesses is a $125 trillion annual market. Most of it still happens via slow, expensive bank transfers. If Visa can move even a fraction of that onto its network, the payment volume grows enormously. Visa has been building products for exactly this purpose. Success here could add $10 to $20 trillion in annual payment volume.
B2B is 10x bigger than consumer payments. Even 5% share would transform the revenue line.
02
Cash disappears faster in emerging markets than expected
India, Southeast Asia, and Africa are still mostly cash economies. If governments accelerate digital payment adoption through infrastructure and policy, the number of transactions on Visa's network grows rapidly. India alone has a billion potential new digital payers. Each one that taps a Visa card adds to the total.
Each percentage point of cash replaced by cards adds hundreds of billions to Visa's total payment volume.
03
The DOJ lawsuit resolves without breaking the core business
The case is live and unresolved. If it settles with modest conditions, the uncertainty overhang on the stock clears. Investors who have been cautious because of the lawsuit would likely return. Visa has survived antitrust scrutiny before. Historical settlements in payments have been expensive but not existential.
Removal of legal uncertainty alone could re-rate the stock meaningfully higher.
04
Value-added services become a second major revenue engine
Fraud tools, data analytics, and consulting are growing at 12% per year. Visa has data on 233 billion transactions a year. Nobody else has that. If they can turn that into a $15 to $20B business of its own, the stock trades on higher earnings at the same multiple and moves significantly higher.
Services revenue at scale means Visa earns even when card volumes plateau.
